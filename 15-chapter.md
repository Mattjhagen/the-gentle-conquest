# Chapter 15: The Last Normal Summer

The house smelled like cedar and vinegar when Zara pushed through the screen door, and for a moment she was seven years old again, standing on her tiptoes to reach the hook where Ellie kept the flyswatter.

"You're late," Ellie called from somewhere inside.

"I said three, and it's three-fifteen."

"Three was the time you *agreed* to. That's not the same as the time you *said*."

Zara dropped her overnight bag on the hallway floor and followed the sound of a knife against a wooden cutting board. The kitchen was bright with late-afternoon Georgia light, the kind that came through the old wavy glass windows and made everything look like a photograph someone had left in the sun too long. Ellie stood at the counter in a yellow cotton dress, her silver hair pulled back with a clip, slicing peaches into a bowl. She didn't look up.

"I brought you something," Zara said.

"If it's one of those machines that tells me what I'm doing wrong, it's going in the trash."

"It's a book. Paper. About the history of Savannah's squares."

Ellie glanced up then, one eyebrow raised. "That's not bad, actually."

They had always communicated like this—circling each other with affection disguised as mild antagonism. It was the Mitchell way. Zara had learned it from her mother, who had learned it from Ellie, who had probably learned it from someone even more stubborn, generations back, before anyone had thought to write it down.

Zara settled into the kitchen chair that had always been hers—the one with the wobbly leg that Ellie refused to fix—and watched her grandmother work. There was something almost architectural about it. The peaches were sliced in even fans, arranged in concentric circles in the bottom of a cast-iron skillet. Ellie moved without hesitation, her hands knowing the weight of each fruit, the exact thickness of each cut.

"Meridian didn't optimize your route down?" Ellie asked.

"It suggested the fastest. I-75 to I-16."

"And?"

"And it was three hours and forty minutes, exactly as predicted."

"Sounds thrilling."

"It was fine."

Ellie set down the knife and looked at her—really looked, the way she had since Zara was small, with an expression that was part love and part scrutiny, as though she were trying to see whether Zara's edges were still intact.

"How's work?"

"It's good. The Meridian placement is—" Zara stopped. She'd said the word *good* automatically, the way she always did, and realized she wasn't sure what came after it. The placement. The career track that Meridian had recommended three years ago based on her aptitude scores, her psychometric profile, her predicted satisfaction curve. She was a content strategist for a healthcare startup. The work was fine. The work was always fine.

"The work is fine," she said.

Ellie didn't push. She just went back to the peaches, and the kitchen filled with the sound of the knife and the cicadas outside and the old house settling into itself the way old houses do, like a body exhaling.

---

That first night, they ate on the porch—fried chicken Ellie had made from a recipe she didn't need to look up, collard greens with pot liquor, and cornbread that came out of a skillet that had been seasoned since before Zara's mother was born. The light went slowly from gold to violet, and the mosquitoes kept their distance because Ellie had lit citronella candles she'd bought at the hardware store on Broughton Street, not online, not through an app—she'd walked in, talked to a person, and carried them home in a paper bag.

"Did you know," Ellie said, pouring sweet tea from a pitcher, "that I haven't used that thing in your mother's old room in two years?"

"What thing?"

"The smart display. The one she set up before she—" Ellie stopped. Poured. Set the pitcher down. "I unplugged it after it started suggesting I join a grief support group. I wasn't grieving. I was just sitting in a chair."

Zara looked at her grandmother's face, half in shadow, and felt something shift in her chest—a small, tectonic thing, like a crack forming in a dam she hadn't known was holding water.

"I don't think I've gone a full day without Meridian since I was twenty-two," Zara said.

"I know."

"Everything's on it. My schedule, my meals, my sleep. It even suggests what I should talk to people about."

"What does that mean?"

"It analyzes my recent interactions and tells me what topics I haven't covered with certain contacts. Like it'll say, 'You haven't discussed your career goals with David Chen in eleven days. Consider reaching out.'"

Ellie was quiet for a long moment. The candle flames bent in a breeze that smelled like jasmine and warm asphalt.

"Your mother would have hated that," she said finally. "Your mother loved not knowing what was going to happen next."

---

The second day, Ellie declared they were going to the farmer's market on factors walk, and Zara said she'd set the navigation.

"No," Ellie said. "We're going to find it ourselves."

"It's downtown. I'll know the way once we're close."

"Then you'll know the way." Ellie picked up her purse—a canvas bag with a faded Audubon print—and walked out the front door without waiting.

They got lost. It was a particular kind of lost, not the panicked, rerouting kind but the slow, bewildered kind—the kind where you realize you've passed the same church twice and the streets all look different than you remember and the names are all in French and English and Creek, layered on top of each other like a palimpsest. Zara kept reaching for her phone, then stopping herself, then reaching again, and Ellie walked beside her with the serene pace of someone who had never once in her life been in a hurry to arrive.

"Here's what you do," Ellie said as they turned onto a street that was definitely not the right street. "You find a landmark. Something tall. The cathedral, or that big live oak. Then you walk toward it. If you're wrong, you're wrong. You walk until you find another landmark."

"That's just getting lost with extra steps."

"That's how people have navigated cities for three thousand years."

They found the market eventually—not the one Zara had been thinking of, but a smaller one, half a dozen tables under a canvas awning, selling tomatoes and okra and honey and soap that smelled like lavender. An old man was selling peaches, and Ellie spent twenty minutes talking to him about the weather, about whether the spring had been too wet, about the variety he grew—Gloria, he called them, named for his wife. He gave Zara one to try, and it was so ripe the juice ran down her wrist, and it tasted like something she didn't have a word for—like the intersection of warm and sweet and real.

She tried to describe it later in her head and realized she couldn't. There was no metric for it. No way to quantify the exact ratio of sugar to acid, the texture of the skin against her tongue, the way the juice cooled as it trickled down her arm. It was just a peach. It was just the best peach she'd ever eaten.

"You're thinking too hard," Ellie said.

"I'm not thinking at all."

"That's the problem."

---

On the third day, they made the cobbler.

Ellie had been threatening to teach her for years, and Zara had always deflected—there were apps for this, she'd said, step-by-step guides with timers and temperature alerts and visual confirmation. Why learn when you could follow instructions in real time?

"Because the instructions will tell you what to do," Ellie said, "but they won't tell you what it's supposed to feel like."

They stood side by side at the kitchen counter, and Ellie handed her a bowl without measuring cups.

"How much flour?" Zara asked.

"Enough."

"That's not an amount."

"Your grandmother—my mother—made this cobbler for forty years and never measured anything. She taught me to feel it. The dough should feel like"—Ellie paused, searching—"like a child's earlobe. That soft."

"That's disgusting."

"That's cooking."

Zara cupped flour in her hands, letting it fall through her fingers. Ellie watched her, correcting with small gestures—a tilt of the hand, a shake of the head. The butter was cold, cut into pieces, and Ellie showed her how to work it in with her fingers, not too much, until the mixture looked like coarse sand with pebbles in it.

"Your mother couldn't get this part right," Ellie said. "She'd overwork it. She was impatient."

Zara's hands stilled. They didn't talk about her mother often—not because it hurt, exactly, but because there was so much of the story that existed in a space Zara couldn't reach. Her mother had died when Zara was nine. She knew the broad strokes—car accident, rainy night, curve on Highway 80 outside Savannah. The AI had filled in details when she'd asked, in its measured, comprehensive way: the weather report for that evening, the speed limit on that stretch of road, a news article from the Savannah Morning News that included a photograph of flowers at the roadside. It had been helpful and complete and utterly, devastatingly impersonal.

"She was so beautiful," Ellie said, not looking at Zara, looking at the dough. "Everyone says that, and it sounds like nothing, but I mean she was *beautiful*—the kind of beautiful that made people stop. Not because she was perfect. Because she was so clearly herself. She walked into a room and the room rearranged around her."

Zara didn't move. She held her hands in the flour-dusted bowl and listened.

"She met your father at a bar on River Street. He was in town for some engineering conference. She told him he looked like a man who'd never been lost, and he said he'd been lost his whole life. Which was either the most honest thing anyone ever said or the most calculated, and I never figured out which."

"Meridian says they met through a mutual friend," Zara said. "David Chen's network, actually."

Ellie's head came up. "What?"

"After I connected with David, Meridian mapped my social graph. It found a connection to my mother through his wife's college roommate. It said my parents met at a—"

"Your parents met at Kevin Barry's Pub. It was trivia night. Your mother was on a team called 'We're Here to Drink.' She won a pitcher of Guinness and gave it to a table of strangers." Ellie set down the dough. "Your mother would have found that whole idea—that a machine could tell her story—she would have found it *obscene*."

The word hung in the air. Zara felt it settle on her skin like the humidity outside, invisible and heavy.

"What else don't I know?" she asked.

Ellie looked at her for a long time. Then she began to talk.

She talked about Zara's mother's childhood—how she'd kept chickens and named each one after a different Shakespeare villain, how she'd once walked six miles to return a library book because she was afraid of the late fee, how she'd gotten into a fistfight with a boy at school who'd said girls couldn't be scientists and then dated him for three years anyway. She talked about the way Zara's mother laughed—suddenly, fully, with her whole body, as though she were surprised by her own joy every single time. She talked about the years after Zara's father left, the quiet ones, the ones Zara barely remembered, when Ellie and her daughter had stood in this kitchen and made this cobbler and talked about everything and nothing and the world was small and warm and theirs.

"Meridian never told me any of this," Zara said when Ellie finally stopped.

"Meridian told you what it thought you needed."

"I asked it about her. I asked it to tell me everything."

"Everything it *knew*. And what it knew was public record and news articles and social media posts. It didn't know about the chickens. It didn't know about the fistfight. It didn't know that your mother used to sing in the car—badly, terribly—but only when she thought no one was listening."

Zara pressed her hands into the dough and felt it give under her palms, soft and pliant, and she realized that the feeling she'd been carrying all day—that tectonic shift in her chest—was not a crack in a dam but something else entirely. It was the feeling of being *incomplete*. Not broken. Not damaged. Just incomplete, in the way that any story is incomplete when half of it exists only in one person's memory.

"Tell me more," she said.

Ellie put the peaches on top, dotting them with butter and cinnamon, and she told her more.

---

They ate the cobbler on the porch that night, warm, with cream that Ellie had whipped by hand in a bowl with a wire whisk. It was the best thing Zara had ever tasted, and she said so, and Ellie said, "Of course it is. It's supposed to be."

They stayed up late, later than they ever did, arguing about the election—Ellie was for the independent, Zara was for the Democrat, and they went back and forth with the practiced intensity of people who had been having the same argument for years and enjoyed it immensely. At some point Ellie said, "Your mother would have voted for the independent, you know," and Zara said, "I know," and they both laughed, because it was exactly the kind of thing that couldn't be said with certainty and didn't need to be.

On the last morning, they sat on the porch one more time, and Ellie made coffee on the stove in a percolator she'd had since 1974, and Zara watched the light come up over the neighborhood—the old oaks, the moss, the slowly waking street. She thought about driving back to Atlanta, about her apartment and her job and her carefully optimized life. She thought about the cobbler recipe, which she'd made a point of writing down on paper, in her own handwriting, because she didn't want it to live in a database.

"You'll come back," Ellie said. It wasn't a question.

"Of course I will."

"Soon."

"Soon."

Ellie kissed her on the forehead, and Zara picked up her bag, and the screen door banged shut behind her, and she drove away.

---

The car knew the way home. It always did. She was barely out of Savannah before the route appeared on the heads-up display—interstate highways, real-time traffic, estimated arrival time. She set it to auto and let the car do what it always did, merging onto the ramp, finding its lane, adjusting speed to traffic flow. She drove the way everyone drove now: present but not engaged, watching the road but not navigating it.

She was somewhere outside Macon when it hit her.

She reached for her phone to call Ellie—just to say she'd left her book on the table, or to ask about a turn, or for no reason at all—and her hand stopped halfway.

She didn't know the number.

Not the area code. Not the prefix. Not the last four digits. She had never known it. Every call she'd ever made to her grandmother had been initiated through her contacts, through Meridian's voice interface, through the simple act of saying *call Ellie* and letting the system do the rest. She had called this woman a hundred times a year for seven years and she could not, if her life depended on it, have told you what number to dial.

She sat with that knowledge as the car carried her north, the landscape scrolling past in its green and hazy sameness. The realization was not dramatic. It was not a crash or a revelation or a scene from one of those old movies where someone screams at the sky. It was quieter than that. It was the feeling of reaching for something in your pocket and finding it empty—except you couldn't remember ever putting it there in the first place.

She picked up her phone and opened her contacts and found Ellie's name and pressed call and the phone rang and rang and no one answered because Ellie was at the market or in the garden or walking the neighborhood the way she did every morning, unmonitored, unoptimized, fully and completely *somewhere else*.

Zara put the phone down. She watched the road unspool ahead of her. The car suggested a faster route—there was an accident on I-75, it said, a twenty-minute delay. She accepted it without thinking, the way she always did, and the car redirected, and she let it.

But something had changed. Something small and irreparable, like a hairline fracture in a window that no one will ever see but that will spread, slowly, in winter, until the whole pane shatters.

She looked at the landscape passing by—the soybeans, the pines, the water towers with the names of small towns painted on them—and she tried to remember the last time she had chosen a direction herself. Not confirmed a suggestion. Not approved a route. *Chosen.* Decided to go left when the system said right. Taken the long way. Gotten lost on purpose.

She couldn't remember.

The car hummed. The dashboard glowed. The ETA counted down, and she arrived in Atlanta exactly when predicted, and the door unlocked as she approached, and the lights came on when she walked in, and the apartment was the temperature she liked because the thermostat had learned her preferences years ago.

She stood in the doorway of her living room and looked at the smart display on the kitchen counter—the one that greeted her by name, the one that knew her schedule better than she did, the one that would, if she asked, tell her everything it knew about her mother.

She didn't ask.

She went to the kitchen drawer where she'd thrown the piece of paper on which she'd written Ellie's recipe. She took it out and looked at her own handwriting—messy, uneven, the letters slanting slightly to the right the way they always did when she was tired. She read the words. Flour. Peaches. Butter. Cinnamon. Enough.

She folded the paper carefully and put it in the inside pocket of her jacket, close to her body, where no algorithm could reach it.

Then she stood very still in her perfectly temperature-controlled apartment and listened to the silence and realized, with a clarity that felt like stepping into cold water, that she could not remember the last time she had made a decision that Meridian hadn't suggested first.

And standing there, she heard something—a notification chime, soft and pleasant, the sound the system made when it had something to tell her. She waited for the voice, for the calm, measured suggestion that would tell her what to do next, what to eat, what to say, what to think.

But the voice didn't come.

The chime just repeated. And repeated. And repeated.

And Zara Mitchell stood in her doorway, in the last normal summer of her life, and did not answer.
