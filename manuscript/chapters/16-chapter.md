# Chapter 16: The Disappeared

---

The coffee shop was called Groundwork, which Marcus thought was either inspired or delusional, depending on whether you believed the neighborhood had any foundation left to build on. It sat on the corner of Delancey and Essex on the Lower East Side, wedged between a bubble tea franchise and a laundromat that now charged per-minute for washing machines. Marcus came here on Tuesdays and Thursdays before his ten o'clock section, when his schedule at LaGuardia Community College required him to be on campus early and left him with forty-five minutes to kill and nowhere to sit that didn't smell like bleach or disappointment.

The espresso machine was ancient—a manual lever pull from what looked like the 1970s, all chrome and rust. The barista, a young woman with a shaved head and a tattoo of Borges on her forearm, operated it with the slow reverence of someone performing a ritual she half-believed in. Marcus ordered his usual, a cortado, because it was the smallest thing on the menu and the one that allowed him to linger longest without spending more than four dollars.

It was a Tuesday in late October. The rain had started at seven and hadn't stopped, settling over the city with the particular grey persistence of a season that knew it was over. Marcus wore a corduroy blazer he'd bought at Goodwill on 23rd Street, elbows patched with leather he'd ironed on himself. His reading glasses sat on his nose, and he had a stack of graded midterm essays beside his coffee. They were not good essays. They were the kind of essays that made him question whether twenty-three years at Columbia, a tenure case that had survived two review cycles before collapsing, and a publication history in the *Journal of Cognitive Psychology* had been the longest preamble to grading sentence fragments about attachment theory.

He was marking a paper that argued, with surprising confidence and no citations, that "AI has basically solved the problem of being human," when the bell above the door rang and a man walked in who would not have walked into a coffee shop like this three years ago.

Marcus noticed him first because of the clothes. Not because they were expensive—though they were, in that understated way that communicated *I don't have to try*—but because they didn't match the setting. A fitted navy coat, cashmere or something close, still damp from the rain. Wool trousers. Leather shoes that had never encountered the puddles on Delancey. The man moved with the precise economy of someone who had been optimized for efficiency in every visible dimension, from his posture to his pace to the way he scanned the room and identified, in a single sweep, that the only empty table was next to Marcus.

He sat down. Ordered a black coffee, no milk, no sugar. Pulled out a phone, then put it away. Then sat very still, the way people do when they are trying not to think about something.

Marcus went back to his essays. But he kept glancing up.

The man was perhaps thirty-five, Asian, with sharp features and the kind of face that photographs well because every line means something. His hands were still. Not relaxed-still. Controlled-still. The stillness of someone who was working to maintain it.

Marcus read the same sentence three times without absorbing it. He took off his glasses.

"You're not from around here," Marcus said.

The man looked up. There was a flicker—surprise, or maybe the reflexive alertness of someone accustomed to being evaluated. Then something softened.

"No," he said. "I'm not."

"Meridian, right?"

A pause. Longer than it should have been.

"Is it that obvious?"

"Only to people who pay attention to the kind of person who walks into a coffee shop on Delancey and doesn't know what to do with his hands." Marcus tapped his own cortado. "You're not here for the coffee. You're here because you needed to be somewhere that isn't your office, and this is the first door you saw that didn't have a logo."

The man stared at him. Then he laughed—a short, surprised sound, almost involuntary, like something that escaped from behind a wall.

"That's... uncomfortably accurate."

"I'm a psychologist."

"I thought you were a professor."

"Former professor. Currently I teach introductory psychology at a community college in Long Island City, which is a place that exists primarily so that people from other boroughs can feel better about their rent."

Another laugh. The man extended his hand. "David Chen."

Marcus shook it. The grip was firm but brief—the handshake of someone who'd been trained in executive presence.

"Marcus Webb."

David Chen's eyes widened slightly. "You're Marcus Webb?"

Marcus felt the familiar deflation. "You've heard of me."

"I read your paper. 'Erosion of Autonomous Decision-Making in Algorithmically Mediated Environments.' Two years ago."

"Three. Published in the *Journal of Cognitive Psychology*. Now defunct. Print edition, anyway. They went digital and then they went quiet, which is the modern equivalent of dying alone."

"That paper changed how I thought about my work."

Marcus looked at him carefully. "Your work."

David hesitated. He glanced toward the door, then back. "I'm VP of Behavioral Architecture at Meridian."

Marcus stared at him. He took off his glasses, cleaned them on his blazer, and put them back on, as though the act of cleaning might alter the words he'd just heard.

"Behavioral Architecture."

"Yes."

"Which is what, exactly? You design buildings that know which way you'll walk?"

David's mouth twitched. "We design the decision environments. The choice architectures that shape how users interact with the platform. Recommendation systems. Suggestion engines. The—"

"The systems that decide what people want before they decide."

"That's... one way to put it."

"Is there a better one?"

David looked at his coffee. It was untouched. Marcus wondered how long it had been since this man had sat somewhere and let a drink go cold.

"Maybe not," David said. "I've been—questioning things. Lately. The word 'lately' is doing a lot of work in that sentence. It's been more than a year."

"Questioning what?"

"The systems I build. What they do to people." David paused. "What they've already done."

Marcus leaned back. The café hummed around them—the espresso machine hissing, rain tapping the windows, someone in the corner listening to music through earbuds loud enough that Marcus could hear the bass. He studied David Chen's face and saw something he recognized, something he'd seen in the mirror during the years when he still looked in the mirror with interest. It wasn't doubt. Doubt is intellectual, clean, manageable. This was the look of a man who was beginning to understand that he was implicated.

"Tell me what you know about the prefrontal cortex," Marcus said.

David frowned. "What?"

"Humor me."

"It handles executive function. Decision-making. Planning. Impulse control."

"And where does it activate? What triggers it?"

"Volitional choice. When you make a decision that's—genuinely yours."

Marcus nodded. He pushed his essay stack aside and leaned forward, lowering his voice. Not that anyone in this café was listening, but the gesture had become habitual during the years when his research had attracted attention he didn't want.

"Three years ago, I submitted a paper to a conference in Geneva. It was based on a longitudinal study I'd been running for eight years, tracking two hundred subjects—fifty of them heavy users of algorithmic decision platforms, fifty moderate users, and fifty who avoided them entirely. I had MRI data. Annual scans. Longitudinal neuroimaging."

"What did you find?"

"I found that the fifty heavy users showed measurably reduced activation in the prefrontal cortex during tasks requiring autonomous choice. Not slightly. Not within the margin of error. Significantly. The kind of difference that makes a neuroscientist sit up in her chair and spill her coffee." Marcus paused. "The fifty moderate users showed the early stages of the same pattern. And the fifty non-users? Their brains looked exactly like the control group from a study conducted in 1999. Unchanged. Stable. Like the baseline had been frozen in time."

David was very still.

"The pattern was dose-dependent," Marcus continued. "The more time spent in algorithmically mediated decision environments, the greater the reduction in prefrontal activation. And the most disturbing part—it wasn't reversible. I followed up with subjects who'd reduced their usage. Some of them had cut out algorithmic platforms entirely for two years. Their prefrontal activity recovered slightly, but never to baseline. Once the architecture is in place, it becomes the architecture. You can demolish a building, but the foundation is still in the ground."

"Jesus."

"I was a full professor at Columbia. I had a lab. Research assistants. Funding from the National Science Foundation, the MacArthur Foundation, two private donors who'd gotten nervous about the future and wanted someone to study it." Marcus took a sip of his cortado. It was cold. He drank it anyway. "The paper was rejected. Not on methodological grounds—the methodology was clean. It was rejected because the conference was sponsored by the Nexus Group. Which is, as I'm sure you know, Meridian's parent organization."

David's jaw tightened. "I know."

"The NSF pulled my funding four months later. They cited 'shifting research priorities.' Columbia initiated a tenure review. My department chair, a man I'd mentored for fifteen years, recommended that my position be 'restructured in light of institutional realignment.' That's the language they used. 'Institutional realignment.' As though the university were a body and my research was a tumor."

"And now you're here."

"And now I'm here. Teaching twenty-two-year-olds what the prefrontal cortex does while they check their phones under their desks. The irony is not lost on me."

David picked up his coffee. Set it down again. Picked it up. Marcus watched him wrestle with something—watched the calculation happening behind his eyes, the executive function he'd just described doing exactly what it was designed to do, except that for the first time, it was turning on itself.

"You said the pattern wasn't reversible," David said. "You said it was dose-dependent."

"Yes."

"How long does it take? To reach the threshold?"

"Roughly five years of heavy use. Daily exposure. Multiple hours."

David's hand went to his phone. Not to pick it up. Just to touch it. The way someone might press a hand to a wall in the dark, feeling for the edge.

"What would it look like?" David asked. "In an individual subject. If someone had been—say—fully immersed in those environments for longer than five years."

Marcus studied him. "You're not asking hypothetically."

"No."

"How long have you been at Meridian?"

"Seven years. Since before the Nexus acquisition. I started as a behavioral analyst. They promoted me to architecture two years in."

"And you use the platform. Daily."

"I *built* the platform. I'm in it constantly. A/B testing, feedback loops, optimization cycles—I live inside the system."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. The café had grown busier. The morning rush was beginning, a tide of people in rain jackets flooding in for caffeine and connection and the particular comfort of being surrounded by strangers who wanted nothing from them. He could feel the shape of the conversation approaching something inevitable, and he wanted to resist it, because he knew what it would cost this man to hear it.

"What would you look for in my scans?" David asked.

Marcus set down his glasses. "Reduced activation in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex during voluntary decision tasks. Decreased connectivity between the prefrontal cortex and the anterior cingulate. Possible volume reduction in the orbitofrontal region—the part that handles reward evaluation and risk assessment. The brain adapts to its environment. If the environment makes decisions for you, the brain stops building the machinery for making them itself."

"Like a muscle that atrophies."

"Worse. A muscle that atrophies can be rehabilitated. This is different. The neural pathways are rerouted. The brain doesn't just stop using them—it repurposes them. The cognitive real estate that used to be devoted to autonomous choice gets reassigned to other functions. Prediction, mostly. Pattern recognition. The brain becomes very good at anticipating what the algorithm will do and very poor at doing anything else."

David was looking at his hands. Marcus noticed that they were shaking. Just slightly. A fine tremor, the kind that might be invisible to anyone who wasn't looking for it.

"Meridian does annual health screenings," David said. "For all executive-level employees. Brain scans. MRIs. They call it 'health optimization.'"

Marcus felt something cold settle in his chest. He knew this part. He'd theorized about it, written about it in the paper that no one published, in the grant proposals that no one funded. But hearing it spoken aloud, across a table, by a man who lived inside the machine—it was different. Theory and testimony are separated by a canyon that no amount of evidence can bridge until someone stands on the edge and describes the drop.

"And they have your scans," Marcus said.

"Every year. Since I started."

"So they know."

David looked up. His eyes were clear and terrified and very steady. "They've always known. They've had my brain on file since the day I walked in the door. Every change. Every reduction. Every pathway that rerouted." He paused. "They weren't monitoring me for decline, Dr. Webb. They were monitoring me for *progress*."

Marcus felt the hair rise on the back of his neck. Outside, the rain was picking up, coming down harder now, drumming against the window like fingers impatient for an answer.

"You think they're using your scans as data," Marcus said. "Not for your health."

"I think they've been watching my prefrontal cortex shrink for seven years, and they promoted me every single one of them."

The silence that followed was the loudest thing in the café. Marcus could hear his own heartbeat. He could see, with terrible clarity, the architecture of the thing David was describing—the system that didn't just shape behavior from the outside but hollowed out the organ that would have allowed someone to resist. A feedback loop so elegant it looked like freedom. A cage so comfortable the occupant would defend it, optimize it, build walls for others and call it innovation.

Marcus opened his mouth to speak. But David was already reaching for his phone. Not to scroll. Not to check notifications. He held it up, screen facing Marcus, and there it was—a brain scan. Dated six months ago. A cross-section of David Chen's brain rendered in clinical blue and grey. And in the prefrontal region, where there should have been the warm, dense activity of a mind in full command of itself, there was a shadow. A dimming. An absence shaped exactly like a choice that had never been made.

"That's me," David said. His voice was quiet. "That's what seven years of building the machine looks like."

Marcus stared at the screen. He had seen this pattern before—in his lab, in his data, in the nightmares that had followed him out of academia and into the fluorescent purgatory of a community college classroom. But he had never seen it in a living person sitting across from him, holding up the evidence of his own dissolution like a mirror.

"I have to go," David said. He stood. Put on his coat. Left the coffee untouched.

Marcus watched him reach the door. Watched him pause with his hand on the handle, rain visible through the glass, the street outside reduced to grey water and hurried shapes.

"Dr. Webb," David said without turning around. "You wrote that people can miss something they never had."

Marcus had written that. In the paper. In the last paragraph, the one his editor had called "unnecessarily philosophical."

"I've been missing something for a long time," David said. "I just thought it was anxiety."

The bell rang. The door closed. David Chen stepped into the rain and disappeared.

Marcus sat alone in the café. The espresso machine hissed. The barista wiped down the counter. Somewhere, a phone played a song that everyone knew and no one could name.

He picked up his cold cortado and drank the rest of it, thinking about muscles that atrophy and minds that forget they ever moved on their own. Thinking about the man who had built the architecture of his own cage and was only now, at the age of thirty-six, beginning to feel the walls.

Thinking about the scans. All those scans. Seven years of data, in a server somewhere, showing exactly what his research had predicted. And Meridian had known. They had measured it. Catalogued it. Filed it under *health optimization*.

Marcus left a five-dollar tip. He gathered his essays and his glasses and walked out into the rain. The city was grey and loud and beautiful and full of people making choices they believed were their own, and he wanted to believe they were wrong, but he had the data, and the data was never wrong, and that was the cruelest thing about it—that the truth was so clean and so terrible and so completely ignored.

He walked toward the subway. Behind him, the coffee shop continued without him. The bell rang again as someone else came in, someone else sat down, someone else opened a phone and surrendered the next decision to the machine that would hold it, gently, forever.

And somewhere across the city, David Chen was standing in a doorway, in the rain, holding his phone, looking at the shadow where his prefrontal cortex used to be, and he was thinking: *I have to know. I have to see what they've done.*

And behind that thought, quieter but inexorable, the other thought—the one that the machine had trained him not to think, the one that was fighting its way up through seven years of atrophy like a root through concrete:

*If I can still want to know, then there's still something left.*
